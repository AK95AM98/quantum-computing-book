    Chapter 5: Quantum Communication, Cryptography, and Network Protocols¶

    Opening: When Entanglement Becomes a Channel¶

    Quantum computing uses entanglement as a computational resource — to create the interference patterns that amplify correct answers. Quantum communication uses entanglement as a channel resource — to transmit information in ways that are provably impossible with classical channels alone.

    This chapter develops three foundational quantum communication protocols: quantum teleportation (transmitting a qubit using entanglement and classical communication), superdense coding (transmitting two classical bits using one qubit and entanglement), and BB84 quantum key distribution (generating provably secure cryptographic keys using quantum mechanics). We then examine the broader landscape of quantum communication complexity, discuss the surprising impossibility of quantum bit commitment, and assess the current state of quantum networking infrastructure.

    Together, these protocols demonstrate a theme that runs throughout quantum information science: entanglement is a physical resource that can be consumed, distributed, and exploited for communication tasks that are impossible classically — but it is not magic. Each protocol has precise prerequisites, precise costs, and precise limits.

    5.1 Quantum Teleportation¶

    5.1.1 The Problem: Transmitting an Unknown Quantum State¶

    Alice holds an unknown qubit \(|\psi\rangle = \alpha|0\rangle + \beta|1\rangle\) that she wants to transmit to Bob. She does not know \(\alpha\) and \(\beta\) (otherwise she could just send the classical values). Can she transmit the quantum state exactly?

    Classically, transmitting an unknown real number requires infinite precision — impossible in finite communication. Quantally, the situation is worse: measuring the qubit gives only 1 bit (outcome 0 or 1) and destroys the state. Infinite measurements would be needed to estimate \(\alpha\) and \(\beta\) with arbitrary precision, and even then the no-cloning theorem prevents Alice from keeping a copy.

    Teleportation's answer: Using one pre-shared Bell pair and two classical bits, Alice can transmit \(|\psi\rangle\) to Bob exactly — including the unknown phases — while destroying the original at her end.

    5.1.2 The Teleportation Protocol¶

    Resources: Alice holds qubit \(A\) in state \(|\psi\rangle = \alpha|0\rangle + \beta|1\rangle\). Alice and Bob share a Bell pair: Alice holds qubit \(B_A\) and Bob holds qubit \(B_B\), with the joint state \(|\Phi^+\rangle_{B_A B_B} = (|00\rangle + |11\rangle)/\sqrt{2}\).

    The total three-qubit state (Alice's data qubit \(A\), Alice's Bell qubit \(B_A\), Bob's Bell qubit \(B_B\)) is:

    \[|\psi\rangle_A \otimes |\Phi^+\rangle_{B_A B_B} = (\alpha|0\rangle + \beta|1\rangle)_A \otimes \frac{|00\rangle + |11\rangle}{\sqrt{2}}_{B_A B_B}\]

    Step 1 — Alice applies CNOT (control: \(A\), target: \(B_A\)):

    \[\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\left(\alpha|0\rangle(|00\rangle + |11\rangle) + \beta|1\rangle(|10\rangle + |01\rangle)\right)_{A,B_A,B_B}\]
    \[= \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\left(\alpha|000\rangle + \alpha|011\rangle + \beta|110\rangle + \beta|101\rangle\right)\]

    Step 2 — Alice applies H to qubit \(A\):

    \[= \frac{1}{2}\Big(\alpha(|0\rangle+|1\rangle)|00\rangle + \alpha(|0\rangle+|1\rangle)|11\rangle + \beta(|0\rangle-|1\rangle)|10\rangle + \beta(|0\rangle-|1\rangle)|01\rangle\Big)\]

    Collecting by Alice's two-qubit states \((A, B_A) \in \{|00\rangle, |01\rangle, |10\rangle, |11\rangle\}\):

    \[= \frac{1}{2}\Big[|00\rangle_{AB_A}(\alpha|0\rangle + \beta|1\rangle)_{B_B} + |01\rangle_{AB_A}(\alpha|1\rangle + \beta|0\rangle)_{B_B}$$ $$+ |10\rangle_{AB_A}(\alpha|0\rangle - \beta|1\rangle)_{B_B} + |11\rangle_{AB_A}(\alpha|1\rangle - \beta|0\rangle)_{B_B}\Big]\]

    Step 3 — Alice measures qubits \(A\) and \(B_A\) in the computational basis, obtaining one of four outcomes with equal probability \(1/4\) each. The state of Bob's qubit \(B_B\) after measurement:

    Alice's outcome Bob's qubit \(B_B\) Required correction
    $ 00\rangle$ $\alpha
    $ 01\rangle$ $\alpha
    $ 10\rangle$ $\alpha
    $ 11\rangle$ $\alpha

    Step 4 — Alice sends her 2-bit measurement result to Bob via a classical channel.

    Step 5 — Bob applies the corresponding Pauli correction. After correction, Bob's qubit is in state \(\alpha|0\rangle + \beta|1\rangle = |\psi\rangle\) exactly.

    Worked Example 5.1: Teleporting \(|{+}\rangle\)

    Let \(|\psi\rangle = |{+}\rangle = (|0\rangle + |1\rangle)/\sqrt{2}\) (so \(\alpha = \beta = 1/\sqrt{2}\)).

    After Alice's Bell measurement, suppose she gets outcome \(|11\rangle\).

    Bob's qubit is in state \(\alpha|1\rangle - \beta|0\rangle = (|1\rangle - |0\rangle)/\sqrt{2} = -|{-}\rangle\).

    Bob applies \(XZ\): first \(Z\) gives \((|1\rangle + |0\rangle)/\sqrt{2} = |{+}\rangle\) (up to global phase). Then \(X\) gives... wait, let us apply \(XZ\) in the right order:

    \(XZ(|1\rangle - |0\rangle)/\sqrt{2}\): First \(Z\): \(Z|1\rangle = -|1\rangle\), \(Z|0\rangle = |0\rangle\), so \(Z\) maps \((|1\rangle-|0\rangle)/\sqrt{2} \to (-|1\rangle-|0\rangle)/\sqrt{2}\). Then \(X\): swaps \(|0\rangle \leftrightarrow |1\rangle\): \((-|0\rangle - |1\rangle)/\sqrt{2} = -(|0\rangle+|1\rangle)/\sqrt{2} = -|{+}\rangle\).

    Since global phase is physically unobservable, \(-|{+}\rangle \equiv |{+}\rangle\). Bob has perfectly received the state \(|{+}\rangle\). ✓

    5.1.3 Key Features and Constraints¶

    1. No-cloning is preserved: After Alice's measurement, the original qubit \(|\psi\rangle\) is destroyed. Alice's measurement outcome contains no information about \(\alpha\) and \(\beta\) (all four outcomes have equal probability \(1/4\)). At no point do two copies of \(|\psi\rangle\) exist simultaneously.

    2. Classical communication is required: Bob cannot apply the correct Pauli correction without receiving Alice's 2-bit classical message. The quantum state cannot arrive at Bob until the classical bits do — ensuring no faster-than-light communication. The no-signaling principle is preserved.

    3. Entanglement is consumed: One Bell pair is consumed per teleportation. To teleport \(n\) qubits, \(n\) Bell pairs are needed. Entanglement is a physical resource that must be prepared in advance and distributed.

    4. Perfect reconstruction: The teleported state is exactly \(|\psi\rangle\) — not an approximation, not a statistical copy, but the identical quantum state including all phases. This is remarkable: \(\alpha\) and \(\beta\) are arbitrary complex numbers, yet teleportation transmits them without Alice or Bob ever knowing their values.

    Business Implication

    Quantum teleportation is the foundational protocol for quantum networking. In a quantum internet, quantum states (qubits) must be transmitted between network nodes for distributed quantum computing, quantum key distribution, and quantum sensing. Classical repeaters amplify signals by copying; quantum repeaters cannot copy signals (no-cloning). Instead, they use teleportation: pre-distribute entanglement across network segments, then teleport qubits through the network by consuming entanglement and transmitting classical correction bits. The practical challenge — creating and distributing high-quality Bell pairs across long distances before decoherence destroys them — is the central engineering problem for quantum networking. Companies like Quantinuum, Qunnect, and government programs (European Quantum Internet Alliance, US Quantum Internet Blueprint) are actively pursuing this infrastructure.

    5.2 Superdense Coding¶

    5.2.1 The Dual Protocol¶

    Superdense coding is the "dual" of teleportation:

    • Teleportation: 1 qubit + 2 classical bits + 1 Bell pair → transmit 1 qubit
    • Superdense coding: 2 classical bits + 1 Bell pair → transmit via 1 qubit

    Alice wants to send Bob one of four classical messages \(\{00, 01, 10, 11\}\) using only one qubit — but with pre-shared entanglement.

    5.2.2 The Protocol¶

    Resources: Alice and Bob share \(|\Phi^+\rangle = (|00\rangle + |11\rangle)/\sqrt{2}\). Alice holds qubit \(A\), Bob holds qubit \(B\).

    Step 1 — Alice encodes her 2-bit message by applying one of four operations to qubit \(A\):

    Message Alice applies Resulting joint state
    \(00\) \(I\) $(
    \(01\) \(X\) $(
    \(10\) \(Z\) $(
    \(11\) \(XZ\) $(

    The four Pauli operations map the Bell state to four orthogonal Bell states.

    Step 2 — Alice sends qubit \(A\) to Bob (one qubit transmitted).

    Step 3 — Bob performs a Bell measurement on \((A, B)\) — measures in the Bell basis. Since the four resulting states are orthogonal, Bob identifies Alice's message with certainty.

    Bell basis measurement is implemented by: CNOT (control: \(A\), target: \(B\)), then \(H\) on \(A\), then measurement in the computational basis. This is the inverse of Bell state preparation.

    Worked Example 5.2: Superdense Coding for Message 11

    Alice wants to send \(11\). She holds qubit \(A\) of \(|\Phi^+\rangle = (|00\rangle+|11\rangle)/\sqrt{2}\).

    Alice applies \(XZ\) to qubit \(A\): - \(Z\): \(|00\rangle \to |00\rangle\), \(|11\rangle \to -|11\rangle\). State: \((|00\rangle - |11\rangle)/\sqrt{2} = |\Phi^-\rangle\). - \(X\): \(|0\rangle_A \to |1\rangle_A\), \(|1\rangle_A \to |0\rangle_A\). State: \((|10\rangle - |01\rangle)/\sqrt{2} = |\Psi^-\rangle\).

    Alice sends qubit \(A\) to Bob. Bob now holds both qubits in state \(|\Psi^-\rangle\).

    Bob's Bell measurement: Apply CNOT (control: \(A\), target: \(B\)): $\(|\Psi^-\rangle = \frac{|10\rangle - |01\rangle}{\sqrt{2}} \xrightarrow{\text{CNOT}} \frac{|11\rangle - |01\rangle}{\sqrt{2}} = \frac{|1\rangle-|0\rangle}{\sqrt{2}} \otimes |1\rangle = |{-}\rangle|1\rangle\)$

    Apply \(H\) to qubit \(A\): \(H|{-}\rangle = |1\rangle\). State: \(|11\rangle\).

    Bob measures \(|11\rangle\) → decodes message \(11\). ✓

    5.2.3 The Information-Theoretic Content¶

    Superdense coding achieves 2 classical bits per transmitted qubit — but only when pre-shared entanglement is available. Without entanglement, Holevo's theorem limits classical information transmission to at most 1 bit per qubit. Pre-shared entanglement doubles the classical capacity of a quantum channel.

    This does not violate any conservation law because the entanglement was distributed in advance (at a cost of transmitting one qubit to create each Bell pair). The "2 bits per qubit" is an efficiency of the communication protocol using entanglement as a resource, not a free source of information.

    5.3 Quantum Key Distribution: BB84¶

    5.3.1 The QKD Security Model¶

    Classical key distribution faces a fundamental problem: any classical channel can in principle be eavesdropped without detection. Public-key cryptography (RSA, Diffie-Hellman) solves this using computational hardness — the assumption that factoring or discrete log is computationally infeasible. Shor's algorithm breaks this assumption for quantum adversaries.

    QKD offers a different security model: key distribution that is provably secure based on the laws of physics, not computational assumptions. Even an adversary with unlimited computational power — including a perfect quantum computer — cannot break QKD security without being detected.

    The price: QKD requires a quantum channel (typically a fiber-optic link or free-space optical channel for photon transmission) and an authenticated classical channel (for public comparison of measurement bases). QKD distributes keys, not messages — the message is still encrypted with the resulting key using classical symmetric cryptography (typically AES).

    5.3.2 The BB84 Protocol¶

    BB84 (Bennett and Brassard, 1984) is the first and most widely deployed QKD protocol. It uses four quantum states in two conjugate bases:

    Basis 1 (computational / Z-basis): \(|0\rangle\) (encodes bit 0), \(|1\rangle\) (encodes bit 1)

    Basis 2 (Hadamard / X-basis): \(|{+}\rangle\) (encodes bit 0), \(|{-}\rangle\) (encodes bit 1)

    The protocol:

    1. Alice generates a random string of bits and a random string of basis choices. For each bit, she prepares and sends the corresponding qubit:
    Bit Basis State sent
    0 Z $
    1 Z $
    0 X $
    1 X $

    1. Bob independently chooses a random basis (Z or X) for each received qubit and measures in that basis.

    2. Basis reconciliation: Alice and Bob publicly compare their basis choices (not the bit values). They keep only the bits where both chose the same basis — the "sifted key." This discards approximately 50% of bits.

    3. Error estimation: They publicly compare a random sample of sifted key bits to estimate the quantum bit error rate (QBER). If QBER exceeds a threshold (~11% for BB84), they abort — likely eavesdropping.

    4. Privacy amplification: Classical post-processing (hashing) reduces Eve's partial information to near-zero, producing the final secret key.

    5.3.3 Security Analysis via No-Cloning¶

    Why eavesdropping is detectable: Suppose Eve intercepts each qubit and measures it, then resends a qubit to Bob. Eve must choose a measurement basis. If she guesses wrong (probability 1/2), her measurement disturbs the qubit: for example, if Alice sent \(|{+}\rangle\) (X-basis, bit 0) and Eve measures in the Z-basis, she gets \(|0\rangle\) or \(|1\rangle\) with equal probability, then resends one of these to Bob. When Bob measures in the correct X-basis, he gets the wrong answer 50% of the time.

    Net effect on Alice-Bob correlations: each "intercept-resend" event introduces a 25% error rate on the sifted key bits (Eve wrong basis 50% of the time, and when she is wrong, Bob gets the wrong answer 50% of those times). This is a detectable QBER of 25% — well above the threshold.

    Formal security: Mayers (1996) and Shor-Preskill (2000) proved that BB84 is information-theoretically secure: the mutual information between Eve and the final key is exponentially small, regardless of Eve's computational resources or strategy.

    Worked Example 5.3: BB84 Key Exchange (8 bits)

    Round Alice bit Alice basis State sent Bob basis Bob result Bases match? Key bit
    1 0 Z $ 0\rangle$ Z 0 ✓
    2 1 X $ {-}\rangle$ Z random ✗
    3 0 X $ {+}\rangle$ X 0 ✓
    4 1 Z $ 1\rangle$ X random ✗
    5 0 Z $ 0\rangle$ Z 0 ✓
    6 1 X $ {-}\rangle$ X 1 ✓
    7 0 Z $ 0\rangle$ X random ✗
    8 1 Z $ 1\rangle$ Z 1 ✓

    Sifted key bits (rounds 1,3,5,6,8): Alice = \(00011\), Bob = \(00011\). No errors → QBER = 0%. After privacy amplification, a shorter final key is produced.

    5.3.4 QKD in Practice: Current Deployments and Limitations¶

    Commercial QKD systems are deployed today:

    • Toshiba: Demonstrated cross-state QKD over live US fiber networks (December 2025), achieving key rates sufficient for AES-256 key refresh
    • ID Quantique (acquired by IonQ): Deployed in banking networks across Europe and Asia; quantum random number generators deployed in smartphones
    • QuantumCTek (China): Operates the world's largest QKD network — 4,600 km Beijing-Shanghai backbone with satellite links
    • Eutelsat and SES: Free-space satellite QKD links demonstrating intercontinental quantum key distribution

    NSA/CISA position (2022): QKD is not recommended for protecting National Security Systems (NSS). Reasons: - Requires new physical infrastructure (quantum channels) that cannot share fiber with classical data - Point-to-point only; quantum repeaters for long-distance QKD remain experimental - Authenticated classical channel still needed (does not eliminate PKI) - Hardware vulnerabilities (side-channel attacks on photon detectors, source imperfections) in practical devices - PQC (FIPS 203/204/205) provides comparable security, is software-deployable on existing infrastructure, and is far more cost-effective

    The practical conclusion: QKD offers real, physics-based security advantages for specific high-security applications where infrastructure investment is justified. For most enterprises, PQC migration is the more practical and urgent response to quantum cryptographic threats.

    Business Implication

    The NSA's skepticism of QKD for national security applications should not be read as dismissal of quantum cryptography broadly. For ultra-high-security applications — government communications, financial clearing, critical infrastructure — QKD provides security guarantees that PQC cannot match (PQC security is computational, not information-theoretic). But for most enterprise applications, QKD's cost and infrastructure requirements make PQC migration the appropriate priority. Organizations should allocate their quantum security budgets primarily to PQC (FIPS 203/204/205) and CBOM auditing, with QKD as a future consideration for specific high-value applications.

    5.4 Quantum Communication Complexity¶

    5.4.1 The Communication Complexity Framework¶

    Communication complexity studies how much communication two parties need to jointly compute a function of their distributed inputs. Alice holds string \(x \in \{0,1\}^n\), Bob holds string \(y \in \{0,1\}^n\), and they wish to compute \(f(x,y)\) while minimizing the total bits communicated.

    This framework has broad applications: computing database joins, verifying consistency of distributed datasets, circuit lower bounds. Classical communication complexity often requires \(\Omega(n)\) bits for simple functions.

    5.4.2 Quantum Advantage in Communication¶

    Quantum communication — transmitting qubits and using shared entanglement — can dramatically reduce communication costs. The most striking result:

    Quantum fingerprinting (Buhrman, Cleve, Watrous, de Wolf 2001): For the equality function \(f(x,y) = [x = y]\), the classical randomized communication complexity is \(\Omega(\log n)\) bits. Quantum protocols using \(O(\log \log n)\) qubits achieve the same task — an exponential separation in the quantum communication model.

    The protocol uses quantum fingerprints: \(n\)-bit strings \(x\) are mapped to \(O(\log n)\)-qubit states \(|f_x\rangle\) such that \(|\langle f_x|f_y\rangle|^2 \approx [x=y]\). Alice sends her fingerprint qubit; a SWAP test on the two fingerprints detects equality with bounded error.

    More broadly, quantum communication complexity demonstrates that entanglement reduces the information that must be transmitted for joint computation tasks. This has implications for:

    • Distributed quantum computing: Quantum processors at different locations cooperating on a joint computation can communicate quantum states rather than classical descriptions, potentially reducing communication bottlenecks
    • Quantum network protocols: Applications like distributed quantum sensing (quantum-enhanced positioning, clock synchronization) exploit quantum communication advantages

    For a comprehensive treatment, see Buhrman et al. (2010), "Nonlocality and communication complexity," Reviews of Modern Physics 82(1).

    5.5 The Impossibility of Quantum Bit Commitment¶

    5.5.1 What Bit Commitment Is¶

    A bit commitment scheme allows one party (Alice) to commit to a bit value (0 or 1) — in a way that: - Binding: Alice cannot change her committed value after the fact - Concealing: Bob cannot determine Alice's committed value before she reveals it

    Bit commitment is a fundamental cryptographic primitive, enabling many higher-level protocols (zero-knowledge proofs, secure computation, coin flipping).

    Classical bit commitment is computationally secure at best — its security depends on hardness assumptions. A natural question: can quantum mechanics provide unconditionally secure (information-theoretic) bit commitment?

    Common Misconception: Quantum Doesn't Solve Everything

    Unconditional quantum bit commitment is impossible.

    Mayers (1997) and Lo & Chau (1997) proved that no quantum protocol can achieve unconditionally secure bit commitment — binding and concealing simultaneously, with information-theoretic security against computationally unbounded adversaries.

    The proof uses a subtle quantum argument: if a scheme is perfectly concealing (Bob learns nothing before reveal), then Alice can always cheat using entanglement. The commitment state for bit 0 and the commitment state for bit 1 must have identical reduced density matrices at Bob's side. But then Alice can apply a local unitary to her part of the entangled system to convert the commitment for 0 into the commitment for 1 — breaking the binding property.

    This result is important for two reasons:

    1. Quantum cryptography does not solve all cryptographic problems. The security of BB84 and other QKD protocols stems from specific features of those protocols (no-cloning, measurement disturbance) — not from a general quantum advantage for all of cryptography.

    2. The impossibility proof illuminates quantum mechanics itself. The fact that purifications of the same mixed state are related by local unitaries — the mathematical fact that drives the impossibility — is a deep result about the structure of quantum information.

    For CXOs: claims that "quantum cryptography makes all communications unbreakable" are false. QKD provides provably secure key distribution for specific protocols; it does not provide unconditional security for arbitrary cryptographic tasks. The security guarantee is narrow but genuine.

    5.6 Toward a Quantum Internet¶

    5.6.1 The Quantum Networking Stack¶

    A quantum internet requires building up from physical qubits to quantum application protocols — a quantum analogue of the classical internet protocol stack:

    Layer Classical Internet Quantum Internet
    Physical Electrical/optical signals Photons, matter qubits
    Link Ethernet, WiFi Bell pair distribution, quantum repeaters
    Network IP routing Entanglement routing, purification
    Transport TCP/UDP Quantum error correction over links
    Application HTTP, SMTP QKD, distributed quantum computing, quantum sensing

    The key challenge at each layer:

    • Physical: Photon loss in fiber (~0.2 dB/km) limits direct transmission to ~100 km before the Bell pair fidelity becomes too low for useful entanglement
    • Link: Quantum repeaters (using entanglement swapping and quantum memories) extend range, but quantum memories with sufficient coherence time (\(>\) milliseconds) and high fidelity remain experimental
    • Network: Entanglement routing must match entanglement generation rates with application demand, across a potentially dynamic network topology
    • Application: Most quantum network applications require high-fidelity Bell pairs; applications like quantum key distribution tolerate moderate error rates while quantum computing over a network requires near-perfect fidelity

    5.6.2 Current State of Quantum Networking (2025–2026)¶

    Organization Achievement Status
    Delft University (QuTech) 3-node quantum network with entanglement across 10+ km Operational (research)
    China Mozi satellite QKD over 7,600 km intercontinental Demonstrated 2017
    European Quantum Internet Alliance Quantum repeater testbeds across 5+ EU cities Under development
    US DOE Quantum Internet Blueprint National quantum network architecture Planning/early implementation
    Toshiba Commercial QKD over live fiber in US and UK Commercial deployment
    Qunnect NYC metropolitan quantum network testbed Testing phase

    A fully functional quantum internet — enabling distributed quantum computing and guaranteed-secure global communication — remains a long-term goal (likely 2030+ for initial demonstration, 2040+ for meaningful scale). Near-term quantum networks will support primarily QKD and quantum sensing applications.

    Business Implication

    The quantum internet is not a replacement for the classical internet — it is a complementary overlay providing capabilities impossible classically. Near-term quantum network investments should focus on: (1) QKD infrastructure for highest-security links (financial clearing, government communications), (2) quantum sensing networks (precision timing, gravity mapping), and (3) connecting remote quantum processors for distributed computation. For most enterprises, the quantum internet horizon is 5–15 years out, and investment now is primarily strategic positioning and talent development rather than near-term operational deployment.

    Chapter 5 Summary¶

    This chapter has developed the major quantum communication protocols and their security foundations:

    Quantum teleportation transmits an unknown quantum state using 1 Bell pair + 2 classical bits, destroying the original and perfectly reconstructing the state at the destination. It is the foundational primitive for quantum networking and quantum repeaters.

    Superdense coding transmits 2 classical bits using 1 qubit + 1 pre-shared Bell pair, achieving 2 bits/qubit — double the classical Holevo bound. It demonstrates that entanglement doubles the classical capacity of a quantum channel.

    BB84 QKD generates provably secure cryptographic keys based on the no-cloning theorem and quantum measurement disturbance. It is commercially deployed but not recommended by NSA for national security systems due to infrastructure costs and hardware side-channels; PQC is the practical near-term enterprise solution.

    Quantum communication complexity demonstrates that quantum communication (qubits + entanglement) can be exponentially cheaper than classical communication for specific tasks, with implications for distributed quantum computing.

    Quantum bit commitment impossibility proves that quantum mechanics does not solve all cryptographic problems. The security of specific QKD protocols comes from their specific physics, not from a general quantum cryptographic advantage.

    Protocol Resources Classical equivalent Quantum advantage
    Teleportation 1 Bell pair + 2 cbits → 1 qubit Direct qubit transmission Perfect state transfer without knowing the state
    Superdense coding 1 Bell pair + 1 qubit → 2 cbits Direct 2-cbit transmission 2× capacity with entanglement
    BB84 Quantum channel + authenticated cbit channel PKI/DH key exchange Information-theoretic security
